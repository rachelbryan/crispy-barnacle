    Hydrous ointment (Fig. 1) is the most commonly prescribed emollient amongst our paediatric population. It is of a whitish colour, of moderate greasiness, easy to apply and efficacious as a soap substitute. It is a preparation of dried magnesium sulphate 0.5%, phenoxyethanol 1% and wool alcohol ointment 50%.

    There have recently been two episodes of a dispensing error with hydrous ointment, which we would like to highlight to help prevent similar problems in the future.

    Patient 1. Hydrous ointment was prescribed for a 5‐year‐old boy with atopic eczema. His mother later complained to our paediatric specialist nurse that the ointment she had received had been difficult to apply and had caused considerable discomfort and redness of his skin within seconds of its application. During a home visit, the specialist nurse examined the ‘hydrous ointment’ and found it to be thick, yellow and waxy, such that it would be impractical to apply to the skin. The label confirmed that this was in fact hydrous wool fat 100%.

    Patient 2. A ward patient was prescribed ‘hydrous ointment’ as an emollient for eczema. Prior to the dermatology nurses applying the ointment dispensed by the hospital pharmacy, they noted that it was in fact hydrous wool fat 100% and therefore it was not used.

    The two different pharmacists involved in the dispensing error were informed, and a written warning of this potential source of confusion, due to similar naming of two different products, was circulated to the pharmacies in our local area.

    Hydrous wool fat 100% is used by pharmacists as a base for manufacture of other products. Verbal communication with the manufacturers confirmed that it is never prescribed for topical application in its undiluted form. The similar name of these two products has led to the same error in two different pharmacies. These cases highlight the potential for dispensing errors with hydrous ointment, which were only noticed due to the vigilance of our specialist nurses. We would like healthcare workers to be aware of this potential point of confusion.
